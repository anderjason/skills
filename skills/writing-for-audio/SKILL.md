# Writing for Audio

A listener cannot re-read. That is the whole difference between writing for the ear and writing for the eye, and everything in this craft follows from it. A reader who misses a name backs up and finds it; a listener who misses a name is now hearing every later sentence with a hole in it. The words move past at speaking pace, in one direction, once. So the writing must carry the listener forward without ever requiring them to look back — and the moment it fails, they don't re-read, they leave.

Start with what the sentence can hold. The ear handles about one idea per sentence, so give it exactly that. Keep the subject next to its verb: NPR teaches this with a print obituary whose subject and verb were separated by thirty-seven words — fifteen spoken seconds in which the listener wonders what happened to the man the sentence began with. The radio version was four words: the man's name, then "has died." Say who before what they said — "The mayor said the budget is a disaster," never the print order with attribution trailing after — because a listener can't judge a claim until they know whose it is, and can't scroll back to check. Prefer subject-verb-object, active voice, short words. Fragments are fine. People talk in them.

Compose by talking, not by writing. One broadcaster who wrote nearly three thousand weekly radio talks described the method as talking to the paper, so that the syntax comes out scrambled the way it is in talk. Speak the sentence first, then type what you said. The finished script must then disguise its own existence: perform it with an actor's craft, land it with a friend's sincerity, and calculate any meandering. This is the paradox at the center of the craft: the more written it sounds, the worse it is; the more spontaneous it sounds, the more work it took. Making a ten-minute essay sound like speech is the devil of a writing job.

Write to one listener, not an audience. The highest praise the form gets is that a broadcast sounded like a letter written to you. The BBC's test: how would you tell this story to one person on the Manchester tram? If you wouldn't say it that way, don't write it that way. This is also a register instruction: contractions, plain words, direct address — but no performance of casualness, which the ear detects instantly.

Structure must be audible, because audio has no headings. Everything a page does with layout, the voice must do with words. Open by raising a question and promising an answer — never the inverted pyramid, which answers everything up front and gives the listener no reason to stay. Signpost transitions out loud: "more on that in a little bit" is how a four-hour history episode holds itself together; "Analysis time?" is how the Acquired hosts negotiate their outline audibly so three hours has a felt progress bar. Plant a concrete touchstone early — a person, an object, a number — and return to it; over any real duration it's the listener's landmark. And you may simply announce the landing mid-essay, saying in so many words that the next subject is the one you wish to end on. What reads as clumsy on the page is orientation in the ear.

Withhold something. Attention in audio is the pull of an open question, and the masters manage it deliberately. The Rest of the Story built an entire broadcast form on one concealed fact — everything said was true but incomplete, and the reveal in the last sentence reorganized every sentence before it. Another approach withholds the connection instead: a volcano grounds a rose-packer in Kenya, and only minutes later do you learn this is a story about radar. But know the hazard: that concealed-fact formula was so strong it became a tic, and the same authoritative voice ended up laundering urban legends as fact. Withholding is a loan against the listener's trust; the reveal must pay it back, and it must be true.

Let facts carry the feeling, and never instruct the audience's emotions. This rule appears independently in almost every master studied. One nature-documentary narrator's version: you don't need adjectives, no need to say it's wonderful; a celebrated chase sequence carries eighty-nine words of narration in four and a half minutes, silent through the climax. One documentary storyteller revises each flat declarative sentence a hundred times or more so the interviewees' tape carries all the moral weight. A classic television Thanksgiving story built its emotion from a menu (two kinds of pie, three kinds of cake) and nine children's degrees recited like a litany, then closed by disclaiming the moral outright, telling the audience there were probably no lessons in any of it. The emotion happens in the listener. Adjectives, editorializing, and "amazing" are you feeling it on their behalf, which relieves them of the need to.

Ration numbers, and dress the ones you keep. A listener can't see a figure, can't compare it to the last one, and doesn't carry a calculator. Round hard. One number per point. Buy each one with something felt: 105 degrees is warm enough to kill you, or at least make you pass out, which behind the controls of an airplane amounts to much the same thing. A number without a body is a number the ear discards.

Rhythm is meaning. Vary sentence length deliberately, and put the shortest sentence at the point of maximum weight; one radio election post-mortem lands on a three-word sentence: "The tactic backfired." Pauses are punctuation the listener can hear, and have been scripted so precisely that print editions of the broadcasts had to typeset the ellipses. And when there is something better than your voice — tape, sound, silence, the picture — stop writing. A first lesson taught at CBS: don't write captions, and never let your words fight the pictures, because pictures win every fight. The audio version: write into the sound, let it play, don't say what it already says.

Finish by ear, not by eye. The last edit is always spoken: read the whole script aloud, at performance pace, and fix every place you stumble, run out of breath, or hear yourself sounding like writing. Good audio copy often looks wrong on the page — staccato, fragmented, repetitive. The page is not the medium. If it reads beautifully and speaks badly, it fails; if it reads clunky and speaks naturally, it works.

A note on style: these are foundations, not a house style. The writers behind them range from fully scripted (word by word against timecode) to deliberately unscripted (fixed mileposts with improvised transit, tight but loose), from nine minutes to four hours, from whispered narration to two friends talking. The foundations are what they share. Resist imitating any famous show's surface — the beats, the ironic register, the signature moves; they are oft-imitated for the same reason they're recognizable. Master the constraints instead, and a voice emerges that is yours.

The procedure, then: say it before you write it. One idea per sentence, subject beside verb, source before claim. One listener. Raise a question early and pay it off. Speak your structure. Plant a touchstone. Withhold honestly. Let facts and silence carry feeling. Round the numbers and give each one a body. Put the shortest sentence where it matters most. Then read it aloud and cut everything you couldn't say to a friend.
