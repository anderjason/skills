# Saying Things Simply

Simple is not a property of text. It is a report of the reader's experience.

Joseph Williams, whose *Style: Toward Clarity and Grace* is the best book anyone has written on this subject, makes the point almost in passing: words like *clear*, *dense*, and *direct* "don't refer to anything in those sentences; they describe how those sentences make us feel." This sounds like a philosophical nicety. It is actually the key to the whole craft, because it explains the central paradox of writing: why your own drafts always seem clear to you. You read into your sentences everything you meant by them. The reader gets only the sentences.

Since simplicity is an experience, the craft is knowing what produces the experience. Three things do.

**The grammar matches the story.** A sentence feels clear when its characters are the subjects and their actions are the verbs. "The Federalists argued that popular democracy destabilized government" feels simpler than "The Federalists' argument in regard to the destabilization of government by popular democracy" — not because it is shorter, but because someone is doing something. When the action hides inside an abstract noun (*argument*, *destabilization*), the reader must reconstruct the story you dismantled.

**The familiar comes before the new.** Readers build momentum on words they recognize and spend it on words they don't. Start sentences with what the reader already knows; put the new and complex at the end, where the emphasis naturally falls. This, incidentally, is why the passive voice is sometimes the *simple* choice, whatever the style guides say: it lets you keep the familiar thing up front and hand the reader the strange thing last.

**The words carry images.** George Orwell put a verse of Ecclesiastes next to his own parody of it. The original: "the race is not to the swift, nor the battle to the strong." The parody: "success or failure in competitive activities exhibits no tendency to be commensurate with innate capacity." The parody has *fewer words* — and fifty percent more syllables, zero images, and less meaning. Simplicity is not brevity. It is concreteness the reader can see.

Everything else — every rule in every style guide ever printed — is a technique for producing one of these three experiences. What follows is the working procedure, distilled from the people who practiced it best: Williams, Orwell, Strunk, Richard Lanham, the plain-language editors of government prose, Edward Tufte, and Apple's interface writers. Run it on a draft, in order. The order matters: the early steps delete ideas, the later ones polish sentences, and polishing an idea you should have deleted is wasted work.

## The procedure

**Ask what the reader needs to do. Delete everything else.**

This is the biggest lever, and the one almost everyone — including careful writers — fails to pull. Here is a government paragraph about flood cleanup:

> Flood conditions contribute to the growth and transmission of many kinds of fungi, some of which can cause sickness. Cleanup workers are at increased risk of exposure to airborne fungi and their spores because they often handle moldy building materials, decaying vegetable matter, rotting waste material, and other fungus-contaminated debris. The fungal material is carried into the respiratory tract when airborne particles are inhaled.
> 

A conscientious editor tightens every sentence and preserves every idea. The plain-language editors who actually rewrote it did something else:

> Be careful when cleaning up after a flood. You may be exposed to and breathe in unhealthy mold in: the air; damp building materials; decaying vegetable matter; rotting waste material; any other damp debris.
> 

The causal explanation — why floods grow fungi, how spores reach the lungs — is gone entirely. The paragraph became a warning and a list, because a person shoveling out a flooded basement needs a warning and a list. The rewrite is not faithful to the source text. It is faithful to the reader.

This is the difference between the amateur's cut and the master's cut: amateurs cut words; masters cut ideas. The British government's style guide states the criterion outright: publish only what someone needs to know so they can complete their task. Nothing more.

**Check the first eight words of every sentence.**

Williams' diagnostic is mechanical enough to run on autopilot: underline the first seven or eight words. If you don't find a character as subject and an action as verb, you have a candidate for revision. The usual culprit is the nominalization — an action embalmed as a noun. *Make a decision* is *decide*. *The implementation of the method* is *implemented*. "There will be a stoppage of trains by drivers" is "Drivers will stop the trains."

**Run the paramedic pass.**

Richard Lanham's method for prose already on the table: circle the prepositions, circle the *is*-forms, ask "who's kicking whom," put that action in a simple active verb, and cut the slow wind-up ("The point I wish to make is that…" deletes without loss — try it). Lanham found that ordinary professional prose runs about fifty percent lard, and he insisted on measuring: words removed divided by words you started with. The number keeps you honest about whether you actually cut anything or just rearranged.

**Name things instead of describing them.**

"A method originally used by Skinner and the early scholars in behaviorism" is *Behaviorism*. A description that circles a concept the reader already owns should be replaced by the concept's name. This single move often accounts for half the length difference between a competent rewrite and a great one — and it is easy to miss, because the description feels like diligence.

**Prefer the concrete and the counted.**

Strunk: "A period of unfavorable weather set in" becomes "It rained every day for a week." Amazon's writing culture operationalizes the same rule for numbers: any word two readers could quantify differently is banned. "Significantly better" becomes "+25 basis points." And put statements in positive form — the reader wants to be told what is, not what isn't. *Did not remember* is *forgot*.

**Read it aloud.**

Paul Graham's test, and Apple's: fix everything that doesn't sound like something you'd say to a friend across a desk. With practice, Graham says, "you'll hear the clank as it hits the page." For interfaces and headings, add the front-loading rule — people scan the first words of a line, so "Canteen menu," not "What's on the menu at the canteen today?"

**Delete, then see what's missing.**

Cut past the point of comfort, read the result, and restore only what actually broke. Tufte's version of the same instruction — erase, look, erase again — makes the essential point: this is a loop, not a pass. Nobody produces simplicity in one draft. And accept small losses of precision when the plain claim is true enough; the caveat can live on the details page.

## The same craft everywhere

These moves are not writing tricks. The identical structure appears wherever someone has mastered subtraction, which is decent evidence it is the craft itself and not local convention.

The deletion test, applied per unit: Strunk demands that every *word* tell; Tufte asks of every *mark of ink* what would be lost by erasing it; Dieter Rams says good design is as little design as possible "because it concentrates on the essential aspects, and the products are not burdened with non-essentials." Word, mark, feature — the unit differs, the test is identical.

The honesty constraint: Orwell's "let the meaning choose the word, and not the other way around"; Tufte's lie factor, which requires the effect shown in a graphic to stay within five percent of the effect in the data; Rams' "good design is honest." Simplicity without honesty is just marketing. Every master pairs the two, because subtraction concentrates whatever remains — including any lie.

And one delta worth savoring. In October 2001, Apple's own press release described its new product this way:

> iPod stores up to 1,000 CD-quality songs on its super-thin 5 GB hard drive, and features up to 20 minutes of shock protection…
> 

The same company, the same day, the same product:

> 1,000 songs in your pocket.
> 

Every fact but one deleted. The surviving fact translated out of the engineer's units — gigabytes, ounces — into the units the buyer actually thinks in: songs, pocket. What the reader needs; a character and an action; a concrete image. All three principles in six words.

## When to stop

Two brakes, and they are the difference between simple and simplistic.

Williams: aim for a style "no simpler than our ideas require but also no more difficult than it has to be." Cutting has a failure mode of its own — when a rewrite starts shedding meaning the reader genuinely needs, you have passed simple and entered lossy. The fifty-percent lard factor is a diagnosis of ordinary prose, not a quota.

Orwell, closing his own list of rules: "Break any of these rules sooner than say anything outright barbarous." The rules serve the reader's experience. The moment they stop serving it, the rules lose.

The whole craft compresses to one sentence, and Williams already wrote it: write to others as you would have others write to you.
